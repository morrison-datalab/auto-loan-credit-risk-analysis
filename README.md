Overview
I analyzed over two decades of U.S. auto loan origination and delinquency data from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to see whether subprime lending patterns in the auto market echo what happened in mortgages before 2008. Using period cohort analysis across four credit cycles, I tracked how loan risk composition and delinquency performance have shifted since the financial crisis.
Thesis
The mortgage market got cleaned up after 2008 — regulation forced lenders to tighten standards and the data shows it. The auto loan market didn't get the same treatment. Lenders kept extending credit to subprime and below-prime borrowers at volumes close to pre-crisis levels, cycle after cycle. Now delinquency rates have climbed past 2008 crisis peaks without any actual crisis triggering it. That's not a one-time shock — that's a slow build. And given how much of this debt gets packaged and sold through asset-backed securities, sustained deterioration here could feed into broader consumer credit problems down the line.
Key Findings

90+ day delinquency hit 5.60% in Q1 2026 — the highest point in the dataset, above 2008 crisis levels
Delinquency is up 114% from the 2004-2008 pre-crisis average of 2.61%
$182B in subprime auto loans were originated in 2025 alone
45% of all 2025 auto loan originations went to below-prime borrowers (sub-720 credit score)
Subprime origination volume never meaningfully contracted long-term — it dipped after 2008, then climbed right back

Methodology
I broke the data into four credit cycles and calculated period averages for subprime origination volume and 90+ day delinquency rate:

2004–2008 — Pre-crisis expansion
2009–2013 — Post-crisis contraction
2014–2018 — Recovery and re-expansion
2019–2025 — Current cycle

Data Source
Federal Reserve Bank of New York — Household Debt and Credit Report
Tools Used

Microsoft Excel — data cleaning and cohort analysis
Microsoft Power BI — dashboard visualization
